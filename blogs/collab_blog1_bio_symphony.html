Bio Symphony


Brief: Design and make a bio material instrument and play it together.
Crew: Kyle, Bala, Desire
Duration: 1 Week


The brief sounded very exciting that my group and I jumped into preparing bases for microbes to develop. Anoushka Cole, our bio-expert collaborator directed us to explore bacterial bio-materials for this project and shared recipes to prepare them. We began making them at Kyle’s huge kitchen space. We then mixed various substances into the base to contaminate and let bacteria take it’s home.

Cooking the bases on the hob


Next day we woke up to see no changes on the samples. Little did we know that this process can take upto a week to develop bacteria. We continued preparing samples and tried various ways to speeden the process. We switched the environment from cold to warmer places like bathrooms, kept closer to room heaters, also added swabs from elevator switches, sinks, our mouth, etc. Another day passed expecting for the bacteria to grow.

Image 3 and 4. Black soil and worms found in the park


Third day still had no bacteria in the base. When we met John and Al with our samples, they had suggested we experiment with the existing bases and discover sounds as exposed to various environments, while bacteria tend to grow in the process. There was similar feedback from Ronnie, Anoushka and Tonicha. They had also suggested we take advantage of the existing characteristics like bouncy and flexible nature of the prepared bases and start recording the sounds to explore.

All samples we had in 2 days

Samples over time


Considering designing musical instruments, we were still unsure how to treat the existing materials as we couldn’t anticipate how they would transform. We rather shifted our focus on capturing sounds with the contact mics while interacting with what we had. Kyle had an interesting idea of submerging the contact mics into the materials and capturing sounds from within. To explore this, we created a spherical mould (ball) of Agar Agar Base with the contact mic inside. Fortunately the mic survived the hot mould and the sample solidified within minutes. When we pressed, smashed and hit the ball, we did hear a subtle, unusual sound from within and were quite amused. As the other two bases had different setting time and density, we used the same technique to submerge the mic but changed the activities performed with it. We created tubs of them, added natural substances, where one can mix and squish with hands and captured sounds that were different and new too. With this, we had three instruments to take for the orchestra, the final presentation.

On the D-day, there were two rounds of orchestra performed in class by MA UX, facilitated by Ronnie. It was rousing to see diverse instruments made by everyone with the same starting point. Each group played their instruments one by one and then played together as the room was electrified from random sounds tuning to a great piece of music. Both the groups had a versatile showcase and we sure didn’t see this coming along.


Bio Symphony


My Learnings:

  • I was constantly doubtful about this unique collaboration throughout the process but only after the final presentation, I realized how important the act of trying out helped discover the unseen potential.
  • The group act of playing the instruments together is when the maximum potential was felt. Each small bit added to the final outcome.
