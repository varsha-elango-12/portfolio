Research Phase 2


Literature Review

Alongside conducting workshops and testing ideas, there was quite a lot of reading to understand the already discovered concepts related to my topic. It includes the connection between emotions and the body through existing movement-based theories, awareness about the lived body, interoception and soma, and the factors influencing our movements and behavior. Some interesting insights and evidence are stated below from my literature review.

A few pieces of evidence responding to the research question about how body movements complement expressing emotions beyond facial expressions is pointed out below.

“Emotion is an experience that is both constituted as movement and that at the same time sets us in motion and makes us act.”

Kristina Hook (Serlin,2013)

“We cannot reduce emotion to an appraisal function located in the emotional brain (the limbic system), nor as the six basic emotions, based on studies of facial expressions by Ekman. The facial muscle movements or autonomic nervous system responses are not unimportant; they just fail to account for the whole experience of our own kinetic/tactical-kinaesthetic bodies as we move and are moved by emotion.”

Sheets Johnstone (Höök, 2011)

“There is a generative as well as the expressive relationship between movement and emotion”

Sheets Johnstone (Höök, 2011)

“A purely disembodied human emotion is a nonentity.”

Father of American Psychology, William James (Höök, 2011)

As I read, it delved into the lived body, embodiment, and human interoceptive senses. Some theories and related topics are provided below.

"Lived body is where our mind and body are intertwined."

Mealue Ponty (Aeonmag, 2020)

"As there is never any chance for us to be disembodied, it does not make sense to speak about embodiment. We are always embodied."

Sheets Johnstone (Höök, 2011)

This questions why we describe only certain experiences as embodied experiences from our daily lives like immersive art exhibitions/ experiences, etc. They sure do make me feel different bodily, feel immersed, or internal. But, what about the other kind of regular routine experiences, say drinking a cup of coffee? Even as I drink coffee, I feel energized, active, awakened, and even merrier. So, aren’t these also emotions attached to my body?

This led me to ‘Interoceptions’, which are our sense of the internal state of the body, that’s both conscious and unconscious (‘Interoception’, 2022). Studies show that sensitivity to interoceptive signals can determine our capacity to regulate emotions. The most common examples of interoceptive signals are feeling hungry or thirsty and how we respond to it by intaking food or water to satisfy the sensation.

Kristina refers to Richard Shusterman’s ‘Soma, which is the lived, the sentient, purposive body that implies the essential union of body-mind’ as an alternative term for ‘Embodiment’ to remind ourselves of the 1st person perspective, the unity of mind and body, and the interlinking of our experiences, thinking, and feeling (Höök, 2011) (Shusterman, 1982).

Taking an artist’s perspective of performing with the body, Sondra Hoston Fraleigh talks about how in her dance, the body isn’t an entity/ tool to perform with, but rather the body is the performer itself. (Horton Fraleigh, 2022)

"The body is not something I possess to dance with. I do not order my body to bend here and whirl there. I do not think ‘move’ and then do move. I am the dance. It's thinking is its doing, its doing is its thinking. My dance is my body as my body is myself."

Overall, these along with various other opinions and theories from my reading simply concludes the fact that,

‘Movements, emotions, experiences, and thinking are inseparable entities.'

A Swedish Scientist and Prof. especially known for her work in Somaesthetics and HCI, Kristina Hook (Höök, 2011)

Directed storytelling/ interviews with theater artists and practitioners:

I conducted personal interviews following the method of directed storytelling to understand the importance of the body for a performer, to enact and share stories on stage through various characters and emotions.

All interviews were quite informal and more of storytelling. Below are the questions. There were also follow-up questions spontaneously based on the interviewee’s responses.

  1. Do you agree that the body, excluding the face, is essential in communicating emotions? Why or why not?
  2. How do you pick your motivation and inspiration for enacting an emotion?
  3. How does face masks and costumes help an actor in a performance?
  4. How do face masks and costumes help the audience in a performance?
  5. What are the other reasons for the existence of theater in current days beyond entertainment?
  6. What was the most difficult emotion/ character/ role you have ever performed? Why did you find it difficult?
  7. Has theater affected your personal character/ nature?

Interesting responses and insights from the directed storytelling:

  • It’s important to be aware of my body to communicate who I am.
  • Story drives an emotion, accompanied by the environment.
  • It’s hard for me to act on the facts or opinions that I don’t believe or agree upon.
  • I feel more confident when I have to perform with my face mask. (repetitive from all three interviewees)
  • Mask allows me to detach from myself in my mind, to shift into the role/ character being performed.
  • In theater, I portray to be different human beings, which makes me learn to be a human.
  • With digital and social media being active, we are forgetting to be in our bodies and interact in real-time with fellow humans.
  • Any movement of a human can tell a story, and hold an emotion. It might not be the intended story conveyed by the performer, but the viewer can create a new story out of it.
  • Everyone wants to be a story-teller at this point (referring to films, plays, reels Instagram stories, etc)


Workshop on connecting with our body:

Aim: Inspired by the use of masks in transforming characters in plays, can a faceless mask help one feel detached from their external self to experience their internal self by sensing the interoceptive senses?

To try and let a person feel the freedom of movement in their soma by nullifying their facial identity for themselves, hence to shift focus to the internal body, their emotions, feelings, senses, etc, and doing anything they feel like doing.

Steps:

  • 3 selective participants (well-known people) were chosen to try the workshop.
  • Participants wore masks that covered their heads, but they could see from behind their masks.
  • They were left alone, in an empty, almost dark room for about 12 minutes. For the first 6 minutes, they were accompanied by varying instrumental music, while the rest of the time, they were left alone in silence.
  • They were not guided or asked to move in any way, it was up to them to be the way they want to be.
  • A small verbal introduction on ‘connecting with our body and expressing the feelings’ was read aloud to the participant before the music began to set some context.


Photos from the workshop:

Participants Experiencing


Highlighted feedback/ insights provided by the participants:

  • It was more like meditation. Slowly, halfway through, I felt comfortable in the surroundings.
  • It felt like I’m fluid
  • In silence, I wanted to sit silent and think in my mind.
  • I felt like stretching my body as I felt the strain on my neck and back muscles
  • As I had my face covered, I didn't feel conscious even of the recording camera as I didn't want the whole world to see my random movements
  • The face cover helped me see inside myself, and talk to myself.
  • Darkroom and mask helped me create a blank canvas for my mind to remove distractions and think about my body.
  • My mind was loud over my body.
  • When I have to think, I go into a dark space, so no one is seeing and I can talk to myself.

My learnings from the workshop:

  • The fact of hiding the face in an empty room did allow the mind to only focus on themselves, their movements, and the body.
  • The prolonged 12 minutes helped them to feel comfortable and trust the space, as time passed. It also helped shift focus on their bodies.
  • The space allowed them to pay attention to the body. This lets them feel the muscle strain and pain and is reacted by stretching their body. This was repeatedly seen in all the participants at some point in the course.
  • There was no thinking about how to move the body, it was free-flowing.

Participants described their movements in such a space as ‘random movements’ while sharing their experiences. But, it made me question the,

  1. Why do we do such random movements on a stage like this?
  2. What is coming out of these random movements? Are they really random? Is it unique to an individual or is there a pattern?
  3. What makes you move in such random ways that you don’t want others to see?
  4. What do you benefit from these random movements?
