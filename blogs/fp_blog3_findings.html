Findings - The Direction


The ‘random movement’ that comes out of a person in an alone, judgment-free space, where they move to realize their body even better, isn’t any random movements I assume. It’s coming out of us due to a change in behavior at the mind level. I would like to call these movements, ‘Our Hidden movements’ as we externalize various emotions. Along the process, there is a connection built with the person, to realize their body, to feel the interoceptive senses from their body, that allow such a movement.

We are surrounded by various gazes from society. It influences our ways of life. From walking on public roads, sitting in a restaurant, chatting with friends at a coffee house, attending a conference, dressing up for an event, etc, etc, we move based on so many norms and confinements around us.


Social psychology, as explained by psychologist Gordon Allport, is how the feelings, thoughts, and behavior of individuals are influenced by the actual, imagined, or implied presence of other human beings (Cherry, 2022). Our feelings and emotions are driven by personal actions but situated in this environment. While our movements are widely influenced by society’s gazes, it's essential to be aware of the freedom to move the way we actually want to move. Be it in a private space or public space, restricting our movements can psychologically affect the emotional stability of the body, and suppressing emotions can lead to physical stress on our body. Awareness of bodily movements to externalize our emotions is essential. That doesn't mean that we move to create an uncomfortable environment for our fellow humans, hence mutual respect is always to be taken care of.

“Environment, mind, and body are indivisible”

Feldenkrais (Sankary, et al., 2021)

Under this interest, for the final outcome, I wanted to create an explorative space that invites people to realize their interoceptive senses, by masking their outward projections, as they discover their hidden movements, accompanied by music.


Field Experiment:

Along the process of discovering hidden movements, I wanted to research and test the role of a ‘face-less facemask’ altering a human as it’s one of my design elements. So I conducted a field experiment with 10 people between the 20-30 age group, to narrow down my audience. It included college students and working-class individuals.

How: So, I provided them with 3 different categories of poses to pose that are some natural behavioral attributes but aren’t commonly expressed everywhere and they were Childish, Romantic, and Sexy. It was to see how the same pose changes with and without the mask, without disclosing the use of the mask prior.

Where: I tried it in different spaces like a closed familiar space, open familiar and unknown spaces, roadsides, and footpaths.

Surprise: When I tried it with participant 1, she chose to pose as childish without a mask and sexy with the mask. So, I kept it open for the users to pick what they wanna pose in either of the situations.

Poses from a few participants during the field experiment.


Insights / Findings:

  • 9/10 participants chose to pose ‘childish’ without the mask and 8/10 participants chose to pose ‘sexy’ with the mask.
  • There’s a dynamic shift of character disclosed by people with and without the mask. They chose a comparatively vulnerable pose with the mask.
  • Feedback: They felt that they can push themselves to be the other person they want to be while covering their face (identity). Some form of ‘alter ego’ was coming out through a pose.
  • Although there was freedom of choice, participants still chose to do different poses each time, and it is more expressive in terms of body.
  • The space/ environment altered and restricted certain levels of movements for the participants, especially for the ones in outdoor spaces. They felt being watched even with the mask on.

So, hence, with the no-face facemask supporting the freedom of movement and expression on an individual level, I wanted to next design the environment for the user to discover their hidden movements along with the mask.


Presentation with the faceless face mask:

During the mid-term presentation, I attempted another field experiment on myself where I presented my work wearing the mask in front of the class. This was to test on me how the mask helps me to be freer in expressing my work, as I am personally a shy, stage frightened person.

It’s me, presenting with the mask.


Insights:

  • Not being biased, but I did feel the strength to convey my presentation the way I had prepared over my anxiety, as I was not able to see other people looking at me clearly.
  • It gave me the confidence to just focus on sharing, over observing others’ immediate reactions to what I say and worrying about it.
  • I also felt confident to move my body the way I wanted to, which was quite not the conventional way of my presentation.
  • My thoughts were, ‘if only this mask is invisible to others, I could wear it all day, and feel more confident, although it’s externally altered’.
