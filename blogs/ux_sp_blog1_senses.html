When a Sense is Sensitive...


Brief: Design a tool / instrument that enables you to access the invisible, the unmeasurable and the intangible on streets of London.
Crew: Faiza, Mu Lin, May, Parth, Leon(virtually)
Duration: 1 Week


Since the time I heard the brief, I was confused and equally thrilled. Gathered at a cafe near Bishop Square, my team and I were planning our field research. Faiza suggested we visit London Mithraeum, the ancient temple of Romans to explore the idea of measuring time with objects/places. We agreed as it was just a 10min walk from where we were (London Mithraeum, 2022).

Exploring the streets of London


As we walked, I realized how tireless it felt to walk, looking at people, beautiful building structures, the comfortable pathways and the cold weather! Back in India, I get exhausted by walking with the burning weather and poor walking pavements. This got me thinking on how we can measure tiredness. Can London streets promote walking? With these thoughts, we reached Mithraeum.

We took part in the multisensory spatial experience of a temple ceremony which was a live event over just displaying the found. The use of sound, lights, and scents carried the viewers back in time. Moving ahead, we got into the streets to experiment accelerating the sense of hearing by blind-folding a person as suggested by May.

At London Mithraeum


Each one of us blind folded at different stops and explored ways to accelerate a particular sense of our bodies by obstructing our sense of vision. While gathering insights, it was fun, scary yet explorative. We decided to explore more on sensitivity of hearing and measuring tiredness the next day.

Experimenting senses on streets


With lots of brainstorming, we arrived at a few ideas. One was to let a blind-folded user move towards a specific sound amongst the recreated noisy street, hence accelerating the sense of hearing. Second was to record the sense of tension a blind person faces in a noisy street, using a device like seismograph. We struggled to measure tiredness, so moved ahead to prototype the other two concepts.

Over the weekend, we came across the idea of passive and active echolocation used by blind people on the web, got fascinated and tried quick experiments on it (Be Smart, 2019). We simultaneously prototyped other ideas. Everything was working well and we were to discuss it with John.

Prototyping Ideas


Surprisingly, John disagreed with everything. He questioned why to forcibly blind-fold normal people on streets while experiencing reality? We were so off-track. Further researching, Parth discovered the cocktail party effect, where our brain focuses on one’s auditory attention. It resonated with us on how we turn towards familiar sounds, like our name or mother tongue on streets/parties/etc (Wikipedia contributors, 2022) and so decided to present it. The final idea was to recreate a noisy environment in class, where 12 volunteers were divided into groups of 4, along with us. While having conversion amongst each group, we casually mention one person’s name from another group and if that person hears it, they switch the group. We color-coded the groups, so in the end one can see the colors shuffled, thus the cocktail effect being experienced.

Experiencing the cocktail party effect with volunteers


After presenting, we received very valid feedback like to have introduced the game prior to everyone, to have included tangible things like threads as a visual cue for the audience to understand the cocktail effect, to try and include as many users possible in multiple ways, and to have taken rehearsals before the final presentation.

My Learnings:

  • Firstly, approaching such a brief was very new to me. There was so much learning about how to present, use of mediums, different possible approaches, etc by attempting, seeing everyone’s presentation and being part of the feedback session.
  • I learnt how essential it is to rehearse the simplest thing before getting on a stage.
