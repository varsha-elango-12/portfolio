Can skin starve for a touch?


Brief: Design a way to express skin/world interaction.
Crew Members: Lara, Kristen, Amber, Yixi and Boxun
Duration: 2 Weeks


Week 1

After having a long day with yellow presentations, my new group was still eager to start off the conversation on skin. We had a quick brainstorming over a coffee. The conversations got interesting as we touched upon multiple segments of skin like cultural, fashion, emotional and physical aspects. We documented all of them and decided to choose one post weekend.

Ideation


Getting back at it while connected online, we voted on ‘Memory of Skin’ together. Yixi suggested we visit Ron Mueck’s exhibition on sculptures exposing the skin and body to represent Shakespear’s stages of life at London Ely house (Ron Mueck | 25 Years of Sculpture 1996–2021, 2022). As it would be great to have a visual experience focusing on skin and we visited the museum on Tuesday morning. It was splendid. The level of intricacy, details on texturing, colour tones, even a strand of hair felt so realistic and accurate. We moved to a cafe to refresh and discuss our topic.

At London Ely House


As we began ideation, Amber spoke about how skin behaves and feels differently when touched by different people. For example, how we are comfortable when our partner/ friends hold our hands over a random stranger trying to hold them. It was interesting to see how psychologically our brain sends signals to our skin to accept or deny a touch. We ideated on building experience on the same.

We explored different materials to represent different kinds of touches and reactions a skin goes through. Materials that are soft, warm and inviting like cotton, wool can represent a loving touch while rough, porky ones like wires, pins can represent uncomfortable strangers’ touches. Touch from intermediate groups like friends or family were related with materials like sponge, sand paper, cardboard, etc. We decided to make three suits to be worn on a mannequin, one over the other, based on different body parts and touches and let our user touch, remove and interact with it. We planned to design the suits and do the speed-dating the next day.

We were slightly doubtful about the idea and it’s depth of knowledge on the next day at college. Due to time restrictions and the amount of work left, we kept going on with the plan. While Yixi and I were storyboarding slight variations in the idea for speed dating, Amber and Boxin gathered materials from in and around the studio, art shops and 3D lab for the suits. The speed dating ideas brought a small dilemma regarding whether we should make three gloves for three touches on the same body or make three body suits representing body reactions for each touch. As the responses suggested three suits over three gloves, we decided to test with suits for week 1. It got interesting and fun as we kept building the suit. The model was ready for mid-week presentation.

Designing the suits


We had volunteers role-playing as a lover, partner and stranger, and interacted with the mannequin. As a result of the change in roles, we kept removing the layer from outside to inside, and tried to keep the lover layer naked to keep it raw and real. Our feedback was constructive and diverse like to move away from literal translations to abstract representations, to set a scene over setting a fixed person, the role of consent coming into play in all situations, etc. We completely ignored the idea of consent previously. The tutorials post presentation with Al and Tonisha suggested the idea of an extended skin or a design of an armour that changes based on consent and memory of skin.

During mid-week presentation



Week 2

We met after the weekend at college to begin again, only this time to be more critical and sensitive about the topic. We narrowed down to a single kind of touch. We were attached more to the loving touch over the uncomfortable touch. We started role playing the different ways of loving touch randomly. Hugs were the best interactions that portrayed loving touch from our outputs. We thought about how absence of a loving touch can create a starvation of touch to oneself. We realized the need of a hug from a loved one when it was not possible during covid times. How can our skin remember such touch and yearn for one? Can that psychologically affect us? Can our skin be satisfied with extended hugs? We liked the questions that arose and decided to share the concept of extended skin and it’s interactions. We used the ‘AEIOU’ research method to make sure we are defining the factors right and including all possible ones.

A E I O U


Speaking of making an extended skin with materials, Lara was reminded about the ‘Man and art figure’ from the theatre of the Bauhaus by Oskar Schlemmer (Schlemmer, 1961). When we looked into it, we were immediately captivated. Oskar had reflected the history of theatre as the transfiguration of the human body with elements such as forms, shapes, materials, colours, costumes, etc. There have been plays performed by artists wearing costumes that transform the human body with stage costumes (100 years of Bauhaus: Its influence on costume design and how you can use it – Kozzii, 2022). This was our inspiration to make our extended skin/body and create a stage play that portrays some of the physical interactions we have in our daily lives like handshake, hug, kiss, etc.

We designed four unique costumes that emphasise different kinds of materials like balloons, steel, paper and cloth. Our method of designing the costumes was the most fun and constructive part. All suitable junk around the studios was collected and we kept playing around with each of us to see how we transformed one’s body. We finalised the looks and made the four costumes. While Lara and Boxun were the facilitators, the rest of us wore costumes. We then choreographed our moves to present on stage with the Piano Music of the Bauhaus from Steffen Schleiermacher in 1999 (K-Spectacularsurf, 2019) as it suited the theme and movements. I loved how theatre intersected our design.

Prototyping costumes for extended skin


We performed at the wide space in front of the canteen. Firstly the movements were interactions between the characters, that’s us. Lastly, we moved to the audience and tried interacting with them which was spontaneous and funny. During the feedback session, people were curious to know the backstory. Each of us explained them in parts. Lots of appreciation from the audience including our mentors. We didn’t realise that we had only spent around £5 to make all the costumes, which turned out to be a wealth out of waste.

The Final Performance, Video Source: John Fass, 2021.


My Learnings:

  • As we kept wandering to different bits, one of us kept documenting all, which helped us to refer back and bind ideas.
  • The AEIOU tool helped us cover maximum parameters involved in the interactions.
